The "Russian Doll" of AI

  • Artificial Intelligence (AI): The broad concept of machines acting smartly.
  • Machine Learning (ML): A subset of AI. Machines improving with experience (data).
  • Deep Learning (DL): A subset of ML. Using multi-layered neural networks.
  • Generative AI (GenAI): A subset of DL. Creating new content (text, images).

The Paradigm Shift

Traditional Coding

Rules + Data = Answers

You write the logic: if age > 18: status = "Adult"

Machine Learning

Data + Answers = Rules

You give history: "Here are 1000 people and their status." The machine finds the logic.

Key Terminology

The Data

  • Features (X): The input variables. Columns in your Excel sheet (e.g., Square Footage, Location).
  • Labels (y): The output/target. What you want to predict (e.g., House Price).

The Process

  • Training: The process of the algorithm learning from data.
  • Model: The result of training. The "program" that can now make predictions.
  • Inference: Using the trained model on new, unseen data.

Three Main Types of Learning

Supervised

Learning with a teacher. Data has labels (Answer Key).

Unsupervised

Self-discovery. Data has no labels. Finding patterns.

Reinforcement

Learning by trial & error. Rewards and Penalties.

Supervised Learning

The Teacher Metaphor

Imagine teaching a child to identify fruits. You show an apple and say "Apple". You show a banana and say "Banana".

After enough examples, you show a new fruit and ask "What is this?"

Business Use: Fraud detection, Price forecasting, Sentiment analysis.

Supervised Type A: Regression

Predicting Numbers

When the output label is a continuous number.

  • "What will be the revenue next quarter?"
  • "How many minutes will this delivery take?"
  • "What is the probability of failure?"

Supervised Type B: Classification

Predicting Categories

When the output label is a distinct class or category.

  • "Is this email Spam or Not Spam?" (Binary)
  • "Is this image a Cat, Dog, or Bird?" (Multi-class)
  • "Will this customer Churn: Yes/No?"

Unsupervised Learning

The Pattern Finder

We give the model data without labels.

"Here is a database of 10,000 customers. I don't know who is who. Please organize them."

The model looks for mathematical similarities and groups them.

Use Cases:

  • Customer Segmentation (Marketing)
  • Anomaly Detection (Cybersecurity)
  • Recommendation Systems (People who bought A also bought B)

Introduction to Neural Networks

Inspired by the Brain

Traditional algorithms (Linear Regression) are simple math equations.

Neural Networks attempt to mimic the human brain's structure: Neurons connected by Synapses.

They are excellent at finding complex, non-linear patterns.

Architecture

  • Input Layer: Receives raw data (pixels of an image).
  • Hidden Layers: The "Black Box" where magic happens. Weights are adjusted here.
  • Output Layer: The final prediction (Cat vs Dog).

Deep Learning

"Deep" just means "Many Layers"

When we stack many hidden layers, the network becomes "Deep".

This allows the model to learn a hierarchy of features:

  • Layer 1: Detects edges.
  • Layer 2: Detects shapes (circles, squares).
  • Layer 3: Detects complex objects (eyes, ears).
  • Layer 4: Detects faces.

Generative AI (GenAI)

From Discriminative to Generative

Traditional AI: Classifies existing data. (Is this a cat?)

Generative AI: Creates NEW data. (Draw me a cat.)

It learns the underlying probability distribution of the data to generate new samples.

Large Language Models (LLMs)

The Next Word Predictor

Tools like GPT-4 or Gemini are LLMs.

They are trained on massive amounts of text (internet scale).

Fundamentally, they are just asking: "Given the context 'The cat sat on the...', what is the most likely next word?"

Input: "SAP is the leader in enterprise..."
Prediction: "software" (98%), "hardware" (1%)

Prompt Engineering

Coding in English

With LLMs, the "code" is the instruction you write in natural language.

Zero-shot: Asking without examples.

Few-shot: Giving examples of what you want before asking.

This is a critical skill for modern SAP developers using GenAI Hub.

Prompt:

"Extract the invoice number and date from the following text and format it as JSON..."

Step 1: Data Preparation

80% of the Work

Real-world data is messy. It has typos, missing values, and duplicates.

Splitting: We always split data into:

  • Training Set (80%): To teach the model.
  • Test Set (20%): To grade the model (hidden during training).

Step 3: Evaluation Metrics

How do we know it works?

We don't just guess. We use metrics.

  • Accuracy: % of correct predictions. (Good for balanced data).
  • Precision: "Of all the emails we called Spam, how many were actually Spam?" (Quality).
  • Recall: "Of all the actual Spam, how many did we find?" (Quantity).

The Danger: Overfitting

Memorizing vs Learning

Overfitting: The model memorizes the training data perfectly (including the noise) but fails on new data.

It's like a student who memorizes the textbook answers but fails the test when the questions are slightly different.

We want the "Goldilocks" zone: Good fit.

Ethics & Bias

The Enterprise Risk

If your training data is biased, your model will be biased.

Example: If you only train a hiring bot on resumes from men, it will learn to reject women.

In SAP context, this is critical. We must ensure AI is Fair, Explainable, and Transparent.

GenAI Risks: Hallucinations

"Confident but Wrong"

LLMs are designed to generate plausible text, not truthful text.

They can invent facts, cite fake laws, or make up numbers ("Hallucinations").

Mitigation: Grounding (RAG). Feeding the model real company data and telling it "Only answer based on this context".

You build it (using Python!).

Predictive Maintenance

Listening to machine vibrations to predict failure before it happens, saving downtime.
